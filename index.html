Immunization is saving millions of lives every year across the world. Vaccines play a key role in increasing immunity and reducing infection risks by working with the human body’s natural defenses. According to WHO, we now have vaccines to prevent more than 20 life-threatening diseases, helping people of all ages live longer and healthier lives. It is estimated that vaccination prevents about 3.5 to 5 million deaths every year. Further, as evidenced by the recent Covid pandemic, vaccines play a very critical role in prevention and control of infectious disease outbreaks, thereby improving global health security. Overall, they have become an indispensable tool in improving the wellness of humankind.

Though we have come a long way, there is still work to be done. Vaccination is still a privilege and not a basic right for a vast majority of the human population. According to UNICEF 2021 immunization coverage estimates, developing countries with large populations of children contribute significantly to the number of unvaccinated children despite achieving relatively high rates of immunization coverage. For example, India has achieved 85 per cent coverage of DTP3. Still, about 3.3 million children were un or under-vaccinated in 2021 because of its large cohort of 23 million surviving infants. Efforts to raise global immunization levels will require a strong focus on the countries where the highest numbers of unvaccinated children live – while also ensuring that smaller countries with a subset of hard-to-reach children are not neglected.

The goal of our project is to explore the global vaccination trends and understand the hindrances in achieving higher vaccination rates. We also want to identify interesting insights that can help governments and NGOs in improving the effectiveness of vaccination drives.

Did the Covid Pandemic cause a drop in vaccination rates?

  • Preliminary investigation of data shows that the there was a 3% drop in global vaccine coverage numbers between 2019 and 2020.
  • Our hypothesis is that the Covid pandemic caused this drop.
  • To explore this further we plotted coverage of DTP first dose across the different WHO regions
    • Africa
    • Eastern Mediterranean
    • Europe
    • Southeast Asia
    • Western Pacific
  • We decided to explore DTP first dose as it is considered indicative of vaccine coverage in general.
  • Plotting DTP coverage across the WHO regions showed that it was mixed. Some regions had a drop while it was flat in some and it even went up in one region.
  • Flipping it from % coverage to % not covered made it easier to see the trends
  • To get a more concrete idea, we plotted just the difference in coverage between 2019 and 2020 at country level. This showed that the story is not as straight forward. Some countries saw a drop in coverage while some saw big improvements
